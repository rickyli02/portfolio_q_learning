  - rebalance frequency
  - transaction-free vs transaction-cost-aware evaluation
  - market regime segmentation
  - asset-subset robustness

[Claude | 2026-03-29 13:323:00 EDT]
- Here's my review of Codex's brainstorm entries:
- Overall assessment: well-structured and actionable
- Codex correctly identified the sequencing risk — the biggest trap with these ideas is trying to build too many things in parallel before any one path is meaningful. The priority ordering is sound.
- Where I agree strongly:
  - WRDS first among real-data work. The anti-leakage contract framing is exactly right. The hardest part of real-data work isn't ingestion — it's making the train/val/test split + observability contract airtight before any model sees it. Codex's recommendation to nail down the "smallest leakage-safe experiment contract" before building infrastructure is the right order.
  - Synthetic stress ladder before real data. I'd go further: the stress ladder is arguably a prerequisite for real-data work. If you don't know where CTRL breaks on controlled synthetic inputs, you won't be able to interpret real-data failures meaningfully. The staged ladder (time-varying → jump → stoch vol → heavy tail → non-Markov) is the right ordering because each step relaxes exactly one theoretical assumption.
  - Multi-agent deferred. Correct. That's a different repo tier and would require a market simulator, execution protocol, and agent-interaction abstractions that don't exist yet. Low near-term ROI.

- Where I'd push back or add nuance:
  - Trading costs (Idea 6) may belong earlier than position 3. Transaction costs change the nature of the optimization problem, not just an evaluation filter — they affect what a rational policy should do. If the first real-data experiment runs without costs, the results may not be interpretable as portfolio construction signal at all (pure price-impact is dominant in practice). I'd consider elevating this alongside the WRDS path rather than after it.
  - "Market surprise" and "insider trading" as brainstorm concepts. Codex suggests sharpening or dropping "insider trading." I agree — as stated it's underspecified. But the underlying idea (asymmetric information + private signal) is actually tractable as a synthetic toy: one agent observes a latent jump before it resolves. Worth a follow-up design note rather than dropping it entirely.
  - Options/derivatives (Idea 7). Codex frames this as "pick one of four sub-questions." I agree, but would add: the most tractable first version is probably options as state/conditioning inputs (e.g., implied vol surface as a feature), not options as tradable assets. That doesn't require changing the action space.
